Concept and Development

Cockatoo Expression Sheet

This piece, while not the first, was one of the few beginner drawings I completed when learning Krita. I had wanted extra practice using the application so I was sketching out many different ideas of what I could draw. The concept began with this small doodle in the corner of my brainstorming page. I decided to develop it more as I liked the idea of drawing a bird that was not the more 'classical' witch companion like raven, crow, or an owl.

My initial digital sketch(s) then looked like this as I chose early on to make the layout of the whole piece more a character/expression sheet for the bird, with just the added pose with the witch on the end, so that more of the canvas could be utilized. I was inspired a bit by hawk/falcon trainers which is why I gave the witch longer sleeves and gloves. My bird was based off of a sulphur-crested cockatoo, which was the parrot I thought best matched with the pencil sketch I had started out with. After the digital sketches were done I decided on a color scheme and started blocking out where I wanted the colors to go. Over time as I was drawing I altered the pose and outfit of the witch to be less fierce/sharp and instead look more friendly to match with the cockatoo (as they are considered highly social and expressive birds which would suit a more expressive and friendly looking witch.)
As I was still learning how best to color and shade using the application, and I discovered only after I completed the entire artwork that I could isolate layers to more easily shade them, there were some issues I encountered. I had made seperate layers for each individual piece/body part, and then to organize them separated multiple groups of those pieces, and then made even more groups out of those. Which ended up resulting in lots of lagging and difficulty when drawing. So out of everything I learned though this experince, one lesson I know will stick best with me is to merge groups when I am happy with them to conserve file size, and to avoid making seperate shading layers when possible.

Throughout the process I learned a lot, by watching tutorials on the features in Krita and through simple trial and error until I made it work. In the end it was a fun experience to practice using the newer application as well as practice in drawing a person, which is a subject I have wanted to practice more of.
